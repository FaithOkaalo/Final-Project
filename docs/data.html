About the Data

Data Source

The data used in this analysis was obtained from the Health Information National Trends Survey, Cycle 7 (HINTS 7), which was administered by the National Cancer Institute (NCI). HINTS is a nationally representative, cross-sectional survey of U.S. adults aged 18 and older. The survey was conducted between March and September 2024 using a mail and web-based design.This is a publicly available dataset that can be accessed at hints.cancer.gov.

Data Cleaning

Outcome variable — Cancer information seeking

The outcome variable SeekCancerInfo was originally coded as a character variable with values “Yes” and “No”. This was recoded into a binary numeric variable (seek_cancer_info) where Yes = 1 and No = 0 for logistic regression.

Key predictor variables

Information Environment Variables

Two variables capturing the information environment were converted into ordered factors:

  • MisleadingHealthInfo : how much misleading health information the respondent encountered on social media was recoded into misleading_info with five ordered levels: None, A little, Some, A lot, and I do not use social media. The “no social media” category was kept as a separate level rather than treated as missing, because it represents a meaningfully different group of adults who are not exposed to social media health content at all.

  • HealthRecsConflict: how often the respondent encounters conflicting health recommendations was recoded into recs_conflict with four ordered levels: Never, Rarely, Often, and Very Often.

Attitudinal Variables

Two attitudinal variables were converted into ordered factors:

  • CancerTrustDoctor: trust in doctors for cancer information was recoded into trust_doctor with four levels ordered from least to most trust: Not at all, A little, Some, A lot.

  • PreventNotPossible: agreement with the statement that cancer prevention is not possible was recoded into fatalism with four levels ordered from most to least fatalistic: Strongly agree, Somewhat agree, Somewhat disagree, Strongly disagree.

Demographic Variables

Several demographic variables required recoding:

  • Race/ethnicity (RaceEthn5) was converted to a factor and releveled so that Non-Hispanic White is the reference category in the regression model.

  • Education (EducA) was converted to an ordered factor with four levels: Less than High School, High School Graduate, Some College, and College Graduate or More. Less than High School serves as the reference category in the regression model.

  • Income required special handling. The original imputed income variable (IncomeRanges_IMP) had nine detailed income categories. To improve interpretability, these were collapsed into four broader categories: Less than $35,000, $35,000 to $74,999, $75,000 to $99,999, and $100,000 or more. The imputed version of the income variable was used rather than the original because it has only 0.2% missing data compared to 12.4% for the unimputed version. The NCI used a weighted sequential hot deck imputation method to fill in missing income values.

  • Age group (AgeGrpB) was converted to an ordered factor with five levels: 18–34, 35–49, 50–64, 65–74, and 75+. The 18–34 age group serves as the reference category.

  • Sex (BirthSex) was recoded to retain only Female and Male responses. The small number of respondents (n = 44) who responded “Don’t know” were set to missing because this group was too small for reliable estimation. Female is the reference category in the regression model.

  • Cancer history (EverHadCancer) and health insurance (HealthInsurance2) were each recoded as binary factors (No/Yes), with No as the reference category in both cases.

Frustration Variables

Four variables measuring the experience of seeking cancer information were recoded using the recode_hints() helper function to set invalid responses to missing. These variables: frustration_effort, frustration_quality, frustration_hard, and frustration_upset — were only asked of respondents who reported seeking cancer information (approximately 50% of the sample), so they are used only in the follow-up descriptive analysis restricted to information seekers.

Code 
# Helper function
recode_hints <- function(x) {
  x <- as.character(x)
  ifelse(grepl("Missing|error|Error|Unreadable|Inapplicable", x), NA, x)
}

# Clean data
df <- df_raw |>
  mutate(
    seek_cancer_info = case_when(
      recode_hints(SeekCancerInfo) == "Yes" ~ 1L,
      recode_hints(SeekCancerInfo) == "No"  ~ 0L
    ),
    misleading_info = factor(
      recode_hints(MisleadingHealthInfo),
      levels = c("None", "A little", "Some", "A lot",
                 "I do not use social media")
    ),
    recs_conflict = factor(
      recode_hints(HealthRecsConflict),
      levels = c("Never", "Rarely", "Often", "Very Often")
    ),
    trust_doctor = factor(
      recode_hints(CancerTrustDoctor),
      levels = c("Not at all", "A little", "Some", "A lot")
    ),
    fatalism = factor(
      recode_hints(PreventNotPossible),
      levels = c("Strongly agree", "Somewhat agree",
                 "Somewhat disagree", "Strongly disagree")
    ),
    race = factor(recode_hints(RaceEthn5)),
    race = relevel(race, ref = "Non-Hispanic White"),
    education = factor(
      recode_hints(EducA),
      levels = c("Less than High School", "High School Graduate",
                 "Some College", "College Graduate or More")
    ),
    income = case_when(
      recode_hints(IncomeRanges_IMP) %in%
        c("$0 to $9,999", "$10,000 to $14,999", "$15,000 to $19,999",
          "$20,000 to $34,999") ~ "Less than $35,000",
      recode_hints(IncomeRanges_IMP) %in%
        c("$35,000 to $49,999", "$50,000 to $74,999") ~ "$35,000 to $74,999",
      recode_hints(IncomeRanges_IMP) == "$75,000 to $99,999" ~ "$75,000 to $99,999",
      recode_hints(IncomeRanges_IMP) %in%
        c("$100,000 to $199,999", "$200,000 or more") ~ "$100,000 or more"
    ),
    income = factor(income,
      levels = c("Less than $35,000", "$35,000 to $74,999",
                 "$75,000 to $99,999", "$100,000 or more")),
    age_group = factor(
      recode_hints(AgeGrpB),
      levels = c("18-34", "35-49", "50-64", "65-74", "75+")
    ),
    sex = case_when(
      recode_hints(BirthSex) == "Female" ~ "Female",
      recode_hints(BirthSex) == "Male"   ~ "Male"
    ),
    sex = factor(sex, levels = c("Female", "Male")),
    cancer_history = case_when(
      recode_hints(EverHadCancer) == "Yes" ~ "Yes",
      recode_hints(EverHadCancer) == "No"  ~ "No"
    ),
    cancer_history = factor(cancer_history, levels = c("No", "Yes")),
    insurance = case_when(
      recode_hints(HealthInsurance2) == "Yes" ~ "Yes",
      recode_hints(HealthInsurance2) == "No"  ~ "No"
    ),
    insurance = factor(insurance, levels = c("No", "Yes")),
    frustration_effort  = recode_hints(CancerLotOfEffort),
    frustration_quality = recode_hints(CancerConcernedQuality),
    frustration_hard    = recode_hints(CancerTooHardUnderstand),
    frustration_upset   = recode_hints(CancerFrustrated)
  )

Handling Missing Values

The negative values in the HINTS dataset represent different types of missing data such as “Not Ascertained”, “Multiple responses selected in error”, ” Not Applicable”, “Skipped due to survey logic”. All the negative values were recorded to NA before the analysis. The HINTS 7 survey uses a complex sampling design, so to ensure that the estimates of our findings are representative of U.S adults, sampling weights were applied using the final sample weight (PERSON_FINWT0). Standard errors were calculated using 50 jackknife replicate weights (PERSON_FINWT1–PERSON_FINWT50), with a degrees of freedom of 49.

Missingness summary

Overall, the amount of missing values for our key variables was low. The outcome variable (seek_cancer_info) had less than 1% missing data. The race/ethnicity variable had the highest proportion of missing values at 11.5% (n = 840), which may affect the estimates for racial subgroups.All the other variables had less than 10% missing values. The missing values were excluded from analyses on a variable-by-variable basis.

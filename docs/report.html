Who Seeks Cancer Information? Predictors in a Nationally Representative U.S. Sample.

Author

Faith Okaalo

Published

April 15, 2026

Introduction

Cancer remains one of the leading causes of morbidity and mortality in the United States. It is the second leading cause of deaths in the country after heart disease (Siegel et al., 2024). Availability and accessibility of accurate and timely information is necessary for prevention, early detection and treatment of cancer (Budenz et al., 2022; Finney Rutten et al., 2019). The cancer survivor population has been steadily growing over the past years which highlights the need for reliable health information to support treatment decision-making and follow up care. However, the information environment has become more advanced and complex for many to navigate. A survey conducted in 2021 reported that more than half of U.S. adults received contradicting and misleading information on alternative cancer treatment options on various social media platforms (Loeb et al., 2024). Research evaluating available cancer treatment information on social media platforms has found a substantial amount of information that is available online to be incorrect and potentially harmful (Johnson et al., 2022). Most individuals who use social media platforms as their primary sources of information perceive that most of the information to be misleading or false and a huge proportion approximately two-thirds find it challenging to distinguish true and false health information (Stimpson & Ortega, 2023).

Whether exposure to this kind of information encourages or discourages individuals from seeking cancer information or better answers remains a topic to be explored. Disparities in cancer seeking information patterns have been documented and persist in the United States (Budenz et al., 2022; Finney Rutten et al., 2019). The persistent racial disparities in cancer outcomes in the United States are further exacerbated by unequal access to timely information, prevention and early detection (Siegel et al., 2024). It is therefore vital to understand who seeks cancer information and what challenges they face in order to design tailored and equitable health communication interventions.

The main aim of this study is to examine what predicts cancer information seeking. Our research question is: What demographic and attitudinal factors predict whether U.S. adults seek cancer information, and is exposure to misleading or conflicting health information associated with cancer information seeking?

Methods

Data Source

This study used data from the Health Information National Trends Survey, Cycle 7 (HINTS 7), which is a nationally representative, cross-sectional survey of U.S. adults aged 18. The survey was conducted by the National Cancer Institute (National Cancer Institute, 2024). Data were collected between March and September 2024 using both mail and web-based surveys.The final sample consisted of 7,278 respondents. HINTS 7 is representative and uses a complex sampling design with with oversampling of minority populations to ensure adequate representation across the demographic groups.

Measures

The outcome variable of interest for this project was cancer information seeking, which was measured by a single item asking whether the respondent had sought information about cancer in the past year (yes/no). We had two key predictors which were information environment variables: exposure to misleading health information on social media (five categories: none, a little, some, a lot, and does not use social media) and frequency of encountering conflicting health recommendations (never, rarely, often, very often). Attitudinal predictors included our analysis were: trust in doctors for cancer information (not at all, a little, some, a lot) and cancer fatalism, measured by agreement with the statement that cancer prevention is not possible (strongly agree to strongly disagree). Demographic covariates included race/ethnicity, education, household income, age group, birth sex, personal cancer history, and health insurance status. Full details on variable coding are available on the About the Data page.

Statistical Analysis

Our analyses accounted for the complex sampling design of HINTS 7. Survey-weighted proportions were calculated to describe the prevalence of cancer information seeking across demographic groups. A survey-weighted logistic regression model was then fit to identify independent predictors of cancer information seeking. The results as reported as adjusted odds ratios with with 95% confidence intervals. All analyses were conducted in R.

Results

Prevalence of Cancer Information Seeking

Overall, approximately half 51% of U.S. adults reported seeking cancer information in the past year. Table 1 and Figure 1 show the weighted prevalence of cancer information seeking across demographic and attitudinal groups. Cancer information seeking increased with education level among adults with less than high school education to college graduates. A similar positive trend is also seen across income levels, with individuals earning $100,000 or more reporting the highest levels of information seeking. There were also racial disparities with Non-Hispanic Whites having the highest information seeking rates compared to Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black and Non-Hispanic Asian adults.

Regional patterns in cancer information seeking

Figure 2 bellow shows the geographic distribution of cancer information seeking across the 9 U.S. census regions. Overall, the proportion of adults reporting cancer information seeking varied by region and ranged from 48.1% to 54.2%. The highest levels of information seeking were observed in the West North Central (54.2%), Mountain (53.7%), and Pacific (52.9%) regions. Similarly, the New England region (52.3%) also demonstrated relatively high engagement. The East North Central region (50.5%) and South Atlantic region (52.1%) exhibited moderate levels of information seeking.In contrast, lower levels of information seeking were concentrated in the Southern regions, particularly the East South Central region (48.1%), which had the lowest proportion of adults seeking cancer information. The West South Central region (48.9%) and Middle Atlantic region (49.9%), indicating comparatively lower engagement.

Predictors of Cancer Information Seeking

Table 2 presents adjusted odds ratios from the survey-weighted logistic regression model. Figure 3 displays these results visually as a forest plot, with blue points indicating statistically significant predictors (p < 0.05).

Personal history of cancer was a strong predictor of information seeking. Adults who had a cancer diagnosis had over four times the odds of seeking cancer information compared to those without (OR = 4.02, 95% CI: 2.96–5.45, p < 0.001). Frequently encountering conflicting health recommendations was the strongest predictor of seeking cancer information.Compared to adults who never encountered conflicting recommendations, those who encountered them rarely had 3.78 times the odds of seeking information (OR = 3.78, 95% CI: 2.09–6.84, p < 0.001), those who encountered them often had 4.82 times the odds (OR = 4.82, 95% CI: 2.67–8.68, p < 0.001), and those who encountered them very often had 5.59 times the odds (OR = 5.59, 95% CI: 2.90–10.80, p < 0.001).

Higher education was associated with greater information seeking. College graduates had approximately 2.79 times the odds of seeking cancer information compared to those with less than a high school education (OR = 2.79, 95% CI: 1.48–5.25, p = 0.002). Higher income was also associated with information seeking. Adults earning $75,000–$99,999 (OR = 1.48, 95% CI: 1.05–2.09, p = 0.027) and $100,000 or more (OR = 1.44, 95% CI: 1.07–1.94, p = 0.017) had significantly higher odds than those earning less than $35,000. Adults aged 35–49 had higher odds of seeking cancer information than those aged 18–34 (OR = 1.39, 95% CI: 1.03–1.88, p = 0.032). Compared to females, males had significantly lower odds of seeking information (OR = 0.76, 95% CI: 0.62–0.93, p = 0.007). Non-Hispanic Black adults had significantly lower odds of seeking compared to Non-Hispanic White adults (OR = 0.72, 95% CI: 0.54–0.95, p = 0.021), though differences for other racial and ethnic groups were not statistically significant after adjustment.

Notably, exposure to misleading health information on social media was positively associated with seeking cancer information. Those exposed to “a lot” of misleading information had higher odds of seeking than those exposed to none. However this association was not statistically significant. Trust in doctors for cancer information was also not significantly associated with seeking after adjusting for other factors.

Among Seekers: Was the Information Easy to Find?

As shown in figure 5, 59.1% of information seekers expressed concern about the quality of the information they found, making it the most commonly reported difficulty. Additionally, 41.8% found the information too hard to understand, 41.0% reported that it took a lot of effort to find cancer information, and 38.1% felt frustrated during their search. These findings suggest that even among adults who actively seek cancer information, the experience is often difficult and the quality of available information is a major concern.

The Misinformation Paradox

One of the interesting finding from this analysis was the relationship between exposure to misleading health information on social media and cancer information seeking. Although one might expect misleading information to discourage engagement, higher exposure to misinformation was instead associated with greater cancer information seeking. Figure 6 illustrates this pattern. There was a clear increase in cancer information seeking with greater exposure to misleading health information, rising from 30.1% among those with no exposure to 58.4% among those with high exposure. Adults who did not use social media had an intermediate seeking rate of 37.7%. While this association was not statistically significant after adjusting for other factors in the regression model.

Conclusion and Summary

This study used data from the 2024 Health Information National Trends Survey (HINTS 7) to examine what predicts cancer information seeking among U.S. adults. Overall, approximately half of U.S. adults (51.1%) reported seeking cancer information in the past year, though this varied substantially across demographic and attitudinal groups.

The strongest predictor of cancer information seeking was exposure to conflicting health recommendations, suggesting that people actively search for information when they are uncertain. Personal cancer history was also another strong predictor of seeking cancer information.

Education and income disparities in cancer information seeking were evident even after adjusting for other factors in the model. Adults with less education and lower incomes were significantly less likely to seek cancer information, which might be reflective of structural barriers in health information access. One of the most intriguing findings was a “misinformation paradox,” where adults exposed to higher levels of misleading health information on social media were more likely to seek cancer information. This pattern suggests that exposure to misinformation may act as a motivator rather than a barrier, potentially prompting individuals to verify the information they encounter.

Limitations

This study has several limitations.

  1. HINTS 7 is a cross-sectional survey which makes it difficult to establish causal relationships.

  2. All measures were self-reported and subject to recall and social desirability bias.

  3. The race/ethnicity variable had the highest amount of missing values (11.5%), which may affect the precision of estimates for racial subgroups.

  4. The survey captures whether cancer information was sought but does not include details on the quality or source of information found.

Future Directions

Future research could explore the misinformation paradox to understand whether early misinformation exposure leads to sustained information seeking over time. There is a need for interventions targeting low-education and low-income adults to help reduce disparities in cancer information access.
